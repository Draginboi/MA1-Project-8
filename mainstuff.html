Understanding P-Values

The p-value is a measure of how compatible your data is with the null hypothesis. It does NOT represent the probability that the null hypothesis is true or false.

Scenario: Testing a New Drug

Imagine a pharmaceutical company is testing a new drug to reduce blood pressure. The null hypothesis (H₀) is that the drug has no effect, and the alternative hypothesis (H₁) is that the drug reduces blood pressure.

In a clinical trial, 50 participants are randomly assigned to either the drug group or the placebo group. After the trial, the average reduction in blood pressure for the drug group is 10 mmHg, while the placebo group shows no reduction. The p-value is calculated to be 0.03.

Key Points Explained

  • What has a probability of p?
    The p-value (0.03) represents the probability of observing a reduction in blood pressure as extreme as 10 mmHg (or more extreme) if the null hypothesis (no effect) were true.
  • Why does a low p-value indicate the null hypothesis is likely false?
    A low p-value (e.g., 0.03) suggests that the observed data is unlikely under the null hypothesis. This provides evidence to reject the null hypothesis in favor of the alternative hypothesis.
  • What is statistical significance, and why is p = 0.05 used as the cutoff?
    Statistical significance means the results are unlikely to have occurred by chance. The threshold of p = 0.05 is conventionally used because it balances the risk of false positives (Type I errors) and false negatives (Type II errors).
  • Why is p not the probability that the null hypothesis is true?
    The p-value does not measure the probability that the null hypothesis is true. It only measures the probability of the observed data under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true.
